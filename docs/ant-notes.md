# ANT Notes

MeshAnt is inspired by **Bruno Latour** and **Actor-Network Theory (ANT)**.

This does **not** mean that MeshAnt is a direct software implementation of ANT, nor that it aims to translate Latour's work into a closed computational doctrine.  
ANT is not a ready-made specification for software design.

MeshAnt takes ANT differently: as a methodological provocation.

Its most important provocation is simple:

**before deciding what the actors are, follow the traces of what makes a difference.**

That shift matters.

Many contemporary AI agent systems begin by assigning roles to predefined entities: planner, reviewer, coder, manager, user, assistant.  
MeshAnt begins elsewhere. It asks whether agency can be approached through traces, mediations, articulations, frictions, and relations before it is stabilized as a role or a subject.

## What MeshAnt takes from ANT

MeshAnt is especially shaped by the following ANT intuitions.

### 1. Agency is not reserved for human persons

ANT resists the assumption that only human subjects act.

Forms, thresholds, interfaces, devices, rules, documents, metrics, infrastructures, delays, and classifications can all make differences in a network.  
MeshAnt takes this seriously.

It does not treat agency as an exclusively human property, nor as something that belongs only to person-like software agents.  
Instead, it asks how agency may emerge wherever something redirects, translates, blocks, amplifies, or transforms action.

### 2. Actors should not be defined too early

ANT warns against explaining the world by prematurely deciding what the relevant actors are.

MeshAnt follows this warning by giving priority to **traces** over predefined actors.  
Rather than assuming stable entities at the outset, it begins from what leaves marks, shifts relations, or produces observable differences.

Actors are treated as provisional effects of linked traces, not as unquestioned first principles.

### 3. Mediation matters more than simple causation

ANT is attentive to mediation: the ways in which action is translated, displaced, deferred, transformed, or redistributed across heterogeneous elements.

MeshAnt adopts this orientation.

Instead of reducing every event to a human intention or an agent goal, it asks:

- what mediated this?
- what translated it?
- what amplified or blocked it?
- what changed as it moved?

This is why MeshAnt pays close attention to forms, interfaces, thresholds, delays, queues, and other seemingly secondary elements.  
These are often not peripheral. They are where the network is composed.

### 4. Networks are not backgrounds

In ANT, a network is not merely the environment around already-formed actors.

A network is what composes action by linking heterogeneous elements.  
MeshAnt uses **mesh** in a similar spirit: not as a smooth total structure, but as an uneven field of mediations, asymmetries, and provisional connections.

The point is not to place actors inside a pre-given world.  
It is to examine how worlds are composed through linked articulations.

### 5. Observation is situated

ANT does not offer a neutral, all-seeing vantage point.

Descriptions are themselves interventions.  
To describe a network is already to perform a cut: to highlight some connections, ignore others, stabilize some entities, and leave others unformed.

MeshAnt carries this into its design philosophy.

It does not assume that the system designer stands outside the mesh.  
Every schema, boundary, and unit of analysis is already a situated intervention.

## What MeshAnt does not claim

MeshAnt does **not** claim to be:

- a faithful implementation of ANT
- a complete translation of Latour into software
- a final ontology of actors, networks, or agency
- a neutral mirror of the real world

A simulation does not simply reflect reality.  
It composes a world by arranging traces, boundaries, mediations, and observation positions into a provisional configuration.

MeshAnt therefore treats simulation not as transparent representation, but as **world composition**.

## Why "trace-first"?

The phrase **trace-first** is central to MeshAnt.

It marks a methodological choice:

- do not begin from fixed actors
- do not assume roles too quickly
- do not reduce change to intention
- do not treat boundaries as given
- do begin from what leaves a trace
- do ask what made a difference
- do follow mediations
- do allow actors to emerge provisionally from the mesh

This is not only a theoretical preference.  
It also suggests a different way to build computational systems.

Instead of starting with:
- agent definitions
- role taxonomies
- fixed subject positions

MeshAnt asks whether one can start from:
- traces
- transformations
- asymmetries
- frictions
- thresholds
- articulations
- re-articulations

## On articulation

MeshAnt uses the word **articulation** to name the way something becomes distinct, linked, and consequential within a network.

This term matters because MeshAnt does not treat actors as self-contained substances.  
An actor is not simply "there" from the beginning.

Something becomes actor-like by being articulated:
- separated from a background
- linked to other elements
- made visible in a certain way
- rendered consequential through a chain of mediations

Articulation is therefore prior to stable identity.

## On re-articulation

If articulation produces provisional actors, then **re-articulation** must remain possible.

Actors may split, merge, dissolve, or be redescribed as the mesh changes.  
The boundary that once seemed natural may later appear partial, strategic, or mistaken.

MeshAnt treats this revisability as a design principle.

## MeshAnt's relation to ANT

MeshAnt is best understood as an experimental computational translation of several ANT intuitions:

- follow traces before stabilizing actors
- treat mediation as central
- allow non-human difference-makers into the description
- refuse the premature privilege of a god's-eye view
- understand boundaries as cuts, not givens

This is a translation, not a repetition.

MeshAnt does not attempt to settle ANT in code.  
It uses ANT as a way to resist the reduction of agency to human-like roles and to open a different path for simulations, schemas, and agent systems.

## A practical summary

If a conventional agent framework begins with:

> here are the agents, here are their roles, here are their goals

MeshAnt asks first:

> what leaves traces here?  
> what makes a difference?  
> what mediates what?  
> what becomes visible or invisible?  
> how was this cut made?  
> under what conditions does something become actor-like?

That is the sense in which MeshAnt is ANT-inspired.

It does not begin from the actor.  
It begins from the mesh of traces through which actors are provisionally composed.
