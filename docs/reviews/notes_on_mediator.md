# Notes on Mediator / Intermediary / Actor as Conditional Readings in MeshAnt

This is a design note for MeshAnt.

The current challenge is not only whether `mediator`, `intermediary`, or even `actor` should appear in the framework, but **how** they should appear without collapsing back into a hidden god’s-eye classification.

## Core point

MeshAnt should **not** implement `mediator` / `intermediary` / `actor` as essential properties of things.

In a Latourian sense, something is not simply a mediator or intermediary by nature.
It appears as such **within a chain, under a cut, relative to a perspective and a criterion of comparison**.

So the task is not:

- “decide what this thing really is”

but rather:

- **under what conditions does this element behave as mediator-like?**
- **under what conditions does it behave as intermediary-like?**
- **under what conditions does it become actor-like?**

This means MeshAnt should focus on **conditions of appearance and conditions of reading**, not essence.

---

## Why observer-position alone is not enough

The current framework already treats observer-position as important, and that is correct.

But observer-position alone is probably not sufficient for distinguishing mediator vs intermediary.

What is also needed is something like an **equivalence criterion**.

In other words:

- what counts as “the same” across a passage?
- what counts as “preserved”?
- what counts as “changed enough” to matter?
- what kind of displacement counts as a transformation rather than a relay?

This matters because the distinction is not only “who is looking,” but also:

- **what is being compared**
- **what level of continuity or discontinuity matters under this cut**

For example:

- if a value passes unchanged from A to B, that may look intermediary-like under one cut
- if the same value is reformatted into a category that changes a decision path, that may look mediator-like under another cut
- if that category becomes an institutional obligation or public order, that may be translation-like

So the framework may eventually need cuts to include not only:
- observer-position
- time-window

but conceptually also:
- **equivalence criterion**
- **relevance criterion**

---

## A possible design orientation

MeshAnt should avoid returning statements like:

- “N is a mediator”
- “N is an intermediary”
- “N is an actor”

as if these were final truths.

A better form would be something like:

- **Under cut C, node N behaves as mediator-like relative to trace set T and criterion E**
- **Under cut D, the same node behaves as intermediary-like because operational equivalence is treated as preserved**
- **Under cut E, this chain becomes actor-like because multiple traces stabilize it as consequential**

This preserves the framework’s anti-essentialist and trace-first commitments.

---

## A useful working distinction

A possible practical distinction would be:

- **Intermediary**
  Something that, under a given cut and equivalence criterion, appears to pass along or preserve what matters without significantly reformatting it.

- **Mediator**
  Something that, under a given cut and equivalence criterion, appears to alter, redirect, reweight, amplify, block, or deform what matters in passage.

- **Translation**
  A stronger case in which what passes is not only altered, but converted into another operational regime, vocabulary, obligation, or alignment.

This should not be treated as a universal ontology.  
It should be treated as a **conditional analytical reading**.

---

## Why this matters for MeshAnt

If MeshAnt introduces mediator/intermediary only as user-defined labels, then the framework risks giving up too early and simply outsourcing the distinction.

If MeshAnt tries to automatically classify them as objective facts, then it risks reintroducing a hidden transcendental viewpoint.

The more promising path is in between:

- derive **candidate classifications**
- make the **grounds of classification explicit**
- preserve the fact that another cut may yield another result

So the goal is not full automatic truth-classification, but something like:

**classification with grounds**

That means MeshAnt could eventually produce outputs such as:

- current reading
- alternative reading
- grounds for the reading
- shadow / excluded grounds
- dependence on cut
- dependence on equivalence criterion

This would be much more aligned with the project’s philosophy.

---

## Important implication

The distinction between mediator and intermediary may itself be **situated**.

That is:

- the same node may appear intermediary-like under one cut
- mediator-like under another
- translation-like in a longer chain
- actor-like only after repeated articulation

This is not a bug. 
It is exactly the kind of conditional emergence MeshAnt should be able to show.

---

## Design consequence

MeshAnt should not aim to encode mediator/intermediary/actor as fixed node types too early.

Instead, it should aim to support:

1. **conditional reading**
2. **grounds for that reading**
3. **comparison across cuts**
4. **explicit exposure of the criteria used**

The system cannot eliminate transcendental framing entirely.  
Because it is a framework, it must always define boundaries, insides, and outsides.

But it can avoid pretending that its classifications come from nowhere.

The right move is not to eliminate the cut, but to make the cut and its criteria explicit.

---

## Potential next question

A fruitful next design question may be:

**What is an equivalence criterion in MeshAnt?**

Because mediator vs intermediary may depend not only on observer-position, but on what is treated as preserved, altered, or made consequential across a passage.

That may be the next major conceptual deepening point for the framework.
