# MeshAnt Principles

MeshAnt is guided by a methodological shift inspired by **Bruno Latour** and **Actor-Network Theory (ANT)**:

**before defining actors, follow the traces of what makes a difference.**

These principles are not a closed doctrine.  
They are working commitments for building systems in which agency is not reserved for human-like roles, but can emerge through mediation, articulation, friction, and relation.

## 1. Trace before actor

MeshAnt does not begin by declaring what the actors are.

It begins by asking:

- what changed?
- what left a trace?
- what redirected action?
- what transformed a relation?
- what made a difference?

This is a Latourian wager: do not jump too quickly to actors as if they were already given.  
Follow the traces first.

Actors are approached as provisional effects of linked traces, not as the unquestioned starting point of analysis.

## 2. Articulation before ontology

MeshAnt does not first ask what a thing essentially is.

It asks how something becomes distinct, legible, and consequential within a network.

An actor is not assumed as a stable substance.  
It is approached as an **articulation**: a contingent cut through which some differences become visible, nameable, and actionable.

Ontology is therefore treated as an outcome of articulation, not as its precondition.

## 3. Mediation before intention

Not every transformation should be explained as the effect of a subject with goals.

Rules, interfaces, delays, thresholds, forms, metrics, queues, sensors, and infrastructures can all mediate action.  
They can redirect, amplify, block, translate, or deform relations.

MeshAnt therefore gives analytical priority to **mediation** over anthropomorphic intention.

This does not deny human agency.  
It refuses to reserve agency for humans alone.

## 4. Difference before role

Contemporary agent systems often begin with roles: planner, reviewer, coder, manager.

MeshAnt starts elsewhere.

A role is only one possible stabilization of agency.  
Before assigning roles, MeshAnt asks what is producing differences: translation, filtering, blockage, amplification, delay, classification, thresholding, articulation.

Roles may emerge later.  
They are not the first cut.

## 5. Plural observers before god’s-eye view

No single perspective should define the whole system.

Different observers do not merely look at the same world from different angles.  
They often compose different worlds by rendering different traces visible, relevant, and actionable.

MeshAnt therefore prefers systems that preserve multiple observation positions rather than collapsing everything into one central, totalizing viewpoint.

The mesh is not exhausted by any single gaze.

## 6. Re-articulation before essence

Whatever counts as an actor must remain revisable.

Actors may split, merge, dissolve, or be redescribed as the network changes.  
Boundaries are not sacred; they are effects of articulation.

A MeshAnt system should therefore make room for **re-articulation**: the possibility that an earlier cut was useful, partial, and contestable, but never final.

## 7. Friction, delay, and asymmetry matter

Smooth models often erase what is most consequential.

Delays, interruptions, opacity, unequal access, missing signals, incompatible formats, and uneven thresholds are not external noise.  
They help compose the system itself.

MeshAnt treats friction and asymmetry as first-class conditions of action rather than accidental imperfections surrounding an otherwise pure process.

## 8. The designer is inside the mesh

No system is designed from nowhere.

To define a schema, a boundary, a trace, or a unit of observation is already to intervene.  
Every formalization performs a cut.

MeshAnt does not pretend to escape this transcendental gesture.  
Instead, it seeks to weaken and expose it by recording how cuts were made, from where, and for what provisional reason.

The point is not neutrality.  
It is reflexivity.

---

## In practice

These principles imply a different design habit.

Instead of starting with:

- who are the agents?
- what roles do they have?
- what goals do they pursue?

MeshAnt often starts with:

- what traces can be followed?
- what mediations transform them?
- where do thresholds and delays appear?
- what asymmetries shape visibility?
- under what conditions does something become actor-like?
- how was this cut made, and by whom?

This is not a rejection of agents.  
It is an attempt to loosen the assumption that agency must always begin in person-like roles.

MeshAnt begins in the mesh.
